an intern's apology, 2023-04-19

This was originally written around August 17th, 2018, in a stream of consciousness manner, on my last day in the office.

I had the chance of working in Japan for the summer of 2018. It was a pretty interesting experience altogether but GARBAGE otherwise. I don't understand how these people come into work. I truly don't. I'm looking around the office right now and some people seem interested in what they're doing, but a lot of them just seem to stare into space with a face of hopelessness. A face of just, waiting out their existence. WTF.

This company is run as if every employee (including software engineers, HR, etc) is working at the factory. Mitsubishi doesn't have real IT I think. They could have yubikeys, a network which distributes software remotely, and disconnects offending machines remotely.

Things I have problems with:

  • Work culture:
    • No music
    • Everything is gray
    • Punctuality to an infinite degree. That is, I think if I worked twice as fast as the guy next to me, but showed up 30 minutes late every day, I would be fired.
    • The perception of doing things for safety/security purposes. (I'm not sure if this is a mistranslation of "safety" but things under this umbrella category are frequent)
      • We are required to wear our soft hats when walking outside.
      • Several websites are blocked. pypi.org a lot of github to name some of the more useful websites.
      • They use Windows:
        • Comes preinstalled with secure non-bloatware goodies like Minecraft, Candy Crush, Royal Revolt, etc.
      • I have 2 PC's... One for "research", and one for "office automation" with Outlook, PowerPoint, etc. The research one isn't connected to the Mitsubishi network, so for me to check my emails, I have to physically move to the other computer. However, I have full 100% access to the regular internet using the "research" one.
      • The "research" one has triple monitors with a driving simulator.
      • I also have a raspberry Pi
      • I don't have admin rights to my windows computer. (I had to get permission to install Chrome, Sublime Text, etc)
        • One time, I was needed to install some software to flash a piece of hardware. The entire hierarchy of bosses were at work, but the single sysadmin was not. Thus, I couldn't do anything that day.
      • They don't allow any USB's on campus or on any computers
        • They give stickers to put over the USB ports
      • The guards will chase you down if you're seen walking with your phone out or have any earphones in.
        • It's a safety violation.
      • From the safety manual:
        • "It is not improbable that a colleague in the workplace may suddenly die, leaving permanent scars on family member and the workplace."
        • "Unsafe Behavior: Example No. 10: Do not work long hours while sitting with your legs crossed... Your legs may fall asleep, and if you force yourself to walk under such condition, you may fall."
      • From the Security manual:
        • They seem to have a list of prohibited software. It includes Winny, a P2P file-sharing application that seems to be last updated 14 years ago. Probably common JP software
        • "when discarding purchased items, destroy the item physically with a hammer."
        • a lot of this can be mitigated by switching over to using servers.
  • Work environment:
    • Uniforms are required.
    • Stretches at 3 pm .-.
    • The badges that give you access to the building allow you to badge in/out only once! So if you badge out but stay in the building, you can't badge out again. You'll get stuck in the building...
    • They seem to have meetings that last _ages_ but discuss nothing? I've never seen any code put up during any of the meetings. Always just diagrams and spreadsheets. And they take forever
    • THEY DON'T USE ANY VERSION CONTROL.
      • While I was here a random employee gave a presentation on how to use git & github, and the advantages of it. My boss said he didn't like it. What in the world is going on here. They send code via email. I have to give my code to my boss today, it's my last day, and I'm curious how I'm going to do it because
        • I can't use USB
        • My PC isn't connected to the Mitsubishi network, so I can't send it over email.

Things I think are good:

  • All employees help when cleaning. Some vacuum, some take out the trash, some put new trash bags in, some clean the whiteboards, etc. Everyone helps out.

The worst part is that the country is pretty nice. It would have been fun to go traveling more, but work just burned me out. 1.5 hour commute each way and having to report to the company from 8:30am - 5:00pm was just too much. My lord.

I want to make it clear that I was, and still am, extremely grateful for the opportunity to have worked in Japan. It's an experience I don't think I'll ever have again.
